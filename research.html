Research

From intelligent matter to autonomous scientific discovery

My current work co-designs algorithms, material architectures, and manufacturing processes for adaptive structures and soft robots. My long-term vision is to connect scientific reasoning, simulation, fabrication, and experiments in a governed, evidence-grounded loop.

Explore the research pillars See the long-term vision

Central question

How can we endow matter itself with the ability to adapt intelligently?

The research combines AI-assisted design, adaptive materials, computational mechanics, and multimaterial manufacturing. Rather than optimizing one component in isolation, it treats geometry, material distribution, fabrication, and physical response as a connected system.

Published research

From target behavior to working structure

Four representative studies show the recurring loop: define a physical target, model the response, optimize the architecture, fabricate it, and validate the result.

01 · AI + mechanics

Inverse design of strain fields in hierarchical architectures

Challenge
A prescribed internal strain field is difficult to translate directly into a feasible material architecture.
Approach
A learned forward model and evolutionary optimization connect material allocation to target strain patterns.
Outcome
Designed fields were compared across prediction, finite-element simulation, and experiments, establishing a route from desired response to physical architecture.
Read the paper ↗

02 · Programmable 4D materials

Inverse design of stimulus-driven shape change

Challenge
Arbitrary printed geometries can transform in complex ways that are difficult to plan by trial and error.
Approach
A data-driven forward model predicts deformation while optimization searches material arrangements for a target shape.
Outcome
A general workflow links digital material allocation to programmable transformations in arbitrary-shape hierarchical structures.
Read the paper ↗

03 · Intelligent manufacturing

Integrated extrusion printing of metal–polymer structures

Challenge
Metals and polymers are difficult to integrate because their processing windows and flow behavior differ.
Approach
A dual-nozzle system combines polymers with a low-melting-point alloy processed at 70°C, supported by extrusion and thermal-management studies.
Outcome
The workflow enables multifunctional architectures whose mechanical, energy-absorption, and electrical behavior can be studied together.
Read the paper ↗

04 · Bio-inspired robotics

Origami-based hard–soft robotic grippers

Challenge
A useful gripper must combine compliant adaptation with predictable folding and reliable grasping.
Approach
Origami geometry and hard–soft multimaterial printing integrate rigid links and compliant hinges in one mechanism.
Outcome
The study relates geometric and material parameters to folding mechanics and demonstrates grasping across multiple objects.
Read the paper ↗

Long-term vision · next 5–10 years

A governed research operating system for intelligent physical matter

The goal is not free-form agents exchanging ideas. It is a stateful scientific workflow in which every hypothesis, model, geometry, experiment, claim, and failure remains traceable—and where consequential decisions retain human oversight.

  1. 01DefineGoal, constraints, and evaluation criteria
  2. 02ReasonLiterature evidence and mechanism hypotheses
  3. 03DesignGeometry, materials, simulation, and optimization
  4. 04BuildFabrication plan and physical experiment
  5. 05ChallengeEvidence checks, claim review, and failure analysis
  6. 06LearnReusable artifacts and updated scientific memory
